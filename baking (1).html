A hobby & a happy place

Baking is my love language.

From early morning bread loaves to cookie experiments late at night, the kitchen is where I slow down, get creative, and make something with my hands.

How it started

I started baking in middle school with simple brownies from a box mix and slowly worked my way up to yeast breads, layered cakes, and laminated dough. Every failure taught me something (a very flat loaf once taught me a great deal about yeast), and every success felt genuinely earned.

What baking means to me

Baking is one of the few activities that fully pulls me out of my head. When I'm studying for exams, working on assignments, or navigating a busy week, stepping into the kitchen and following a recipe gives me a sense of calm and focus I don't always find elsewhere. It's methodical, like data science actually. You have your inputs, your process, and your output. And when things go wrong, you debug.

I also love baking as a way to connect with people. There's something about bringing a homemade cake or a bag of cookies to share that just makes people happy in a very immediate, uncomplicated way. Food is community.

Over time I've developed a particular love for bread baking. The way a basic mix of flour, water, salt, and yeast can transform into something with a crackly crust and a soft, chewy crumb is still a little magical to me. I've also been getting into more intricate pastry work: croissants, galettes, tarts. The patience those require is a real exercise in letting go of the need for instant results.

Things I've learned the hard way

  1. 1

    Read the whole recipe first. Every single time. I cannot tell you how many times I've gotten halfway through and discovered I needed to chill the dough overnight.

  2. 2

    Weigh your ingredients. Volume measurements for flour are wildly inconsistent. A kitchen scale changed my baking completely.

  3. 3

    Your oven lies. Get an oven thermometer. Most home ovens run hotter or cooler than they say, and this matters a lot for delicate bakes.

  4. 4

    Room temperature butter is not optional. If the recipe says softened butter, it means it. Cold butter in a creaming recipe will ruin your texture.

  5. 5

    Let bread cool completely. Cutting into a loaf too early releases steam and collapses the crumb. It's the hardest rule to follow and also the most important.
